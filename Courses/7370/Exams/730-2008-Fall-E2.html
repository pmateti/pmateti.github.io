This is a closed book, closed notes exam. Do not give or take help during the exam.

  1. (6*5 points) The following statements may or may not be (fully or partially) valid.   Explain the italicized terms. Discuss/ dispute the statement.  It is possible to write no more than, say, five thoughtful sentences each, and yet receive full score.

    1. Every distributed system must also be a concurrent system.
    2. Asynchronous  message passing is more fundamental than synchronous message passing.

    3. Token-passing algorithms were discussed under the heading of  asynchronous message passing because token-passing does not make sense under synchronous message passing.

    4. The happened before relation, as in the book, requires a logical clock for every process.  But  a typical machine (node) in a distributed system runs several processes. It is sufficient to maintain a logical clock per node that is shared by all processes running on that node.

    5. Peer-to-peer distributed computing is just an academic term for the commercial term client-server computing.
    6. The CSP code [X!2 || Y!3] is equivalent to
      [true -> X!2; Y!3 [] true -> Y!3; X!2]
       
  2. (4*5 points) It is possible to write no more than, say, five thoughtful sentences each, and yet receive full score.
    1. Discuss why it is difficult to implement a semaphore in the truly distributed context.
    2. Discuss if all of the ACKs are necessary in the solution discussed in the lectures.
    3. Discuss if any solution to the distributed mutual exclusion problem can be thought of as a solution to the problem of implementing semaphores in a distributed system, and vice versa.
    4. Describe the problem of "termination detection."
       

  3. (10+20+20 points) In a bag B of integers, it is known that each element appears exactly d times, d > 0. We wish to delete the duplicates resulting in a set of distinct integers that occurred in the bag. Two solutions, one in CSP and another in C-Linda are to be developed.  It is not required that both use the same "idea."  You are expected to present your design and implementation with full explanations.  As usual, we wish to maximize concurrency, we prefer a symmetric solution, we rate correctness much higher than efficiency, and cannot tolerate deadlocks or livelocks.

    1. (5 + 5 points)  Defend why we prefer a symmetric solution (5 points) , and  why  we cannot tolerate deadlocks or livelocks.(5 points) .

    2. (10 + 10 points) [Linda]  Assume that the tuple space already contains all the elements <"B", e> of B, and the size of this bag <"bsz", b>. Unfortunately, the value d is unavailable and needs to be computed by examining the bag B. Fully explain (10 points) your Linda solution design  in English/pseudo-code and on a fresh page implement  (10 points) it in C-Linda. You lose 2 points for each use of inp or rdp. However, the problem may require their use.

    3. (10 + 10 points) [CSP]  The bag B is now generated by a process Q, which outputs P!("dup", d) and then does *[ ... --> P!("B", e) ]. Do not assume that Q generates d duplicates before moving on to the next integer. Develop the process P and any others you wish to have. Fully explain  (10 points) your CSP solution design in English/pseudo-code.  Implement  (10 points) on a fresh page the design in CSP.
